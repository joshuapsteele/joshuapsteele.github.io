INTRODUCTION#

In many conservative evangelical circles, biblical creation theology has been hijacked and eclipsed by the vitriolic debate between Young Earth Creationism and Neo-Darwinism.[1] It is often difficult to see beyond this morass the beautiful tapestry of creation themes in biblical theology. Waltke summarizes the problem well: ‚ÄúInstead of metaphysical questions that shape culture, questions about dinosaurs, a young earth theory, and such dominate the evangelical landscape. This is unfortunate.‚Äù[2] Nevertheless, there is an embarrassment of riches when it comes to Scripture‚Äôs use of creation themes, whether the evangelical community gives them appropriate attention or not. Unfortunately, a comprehensive analysis of biblical creation theology, a field fertile enough to provide lifetimes of work and study, far exceeds the purview of this essay.[3] However, a brief analysis of the motifs of creation as temple, chaos, and redemption will show that the overarching use of creation theology in Scripture is to bring about the praise of the Creator. Biblical creation theology, properly understood, leads to doxology.

CREATION AS GOD‚ÄôS TEMPLE#

A fitting place to begin this study is the creation account of Gen 1:1-2:3, which depicts the cosmos as Elohim‚Äôs holy temple.[4] Although other ancient cosmogonies prominently feature a particular location or building as the nexus for worship of the respective deities, Gen 1 contains no such element. [5] Indeed, other creation accounts in the ANE associated the lack of a temple with the ‚Äúprecosmic condition.‚Äù That is, creation (the expression of divine authority) was not regarded as complete until the temple (the location for the implementation of divine authority) was constructed.[6] The cosmogony of Gen 1 thus seems strange when compared to its ANE counterparts, but this anomaly reveals the main emphasis of the passage: the entire universe is the sacred dwelling place of Elohim.

The structure of the narrative lends itself to this ‚Äúcosmic temple‚Äù interpretation. In the first three days of creation (1:1-13), Elohim calls three ‚Äúspheres‚Äù into being, relating to the main ‚Äúfunctions‚Äù of life: light/darkness (time), water/sky (weather), and land/sea (food).[7] He then proceeds to create in days four through six (1:14-31), assigning the ‚Äúfunctionaries‚Äù of creation their respective roles and spheres.[8] The scene is then set for the climax of the narrative, which is not the creation of humans, but the installation of the Sabbath (2:1-3).

If the connection between this Sabbath rest and creation as temple is not apparent to the modern reader, it is due to a lack of knowledge of where divine rest takes place.[9] Upon reading that Elohim ‚Äúrested‚Äù (Gen 2:2), the ancient reader would have immediately recognized that this was taking place in a divine temple, the exclusively-appropriate place for any god to rest and proceed to rule his domain from the throne.[10] However, the English ‚Äúrest‚Äù does not fully reflect the meaning of the Hebrew ‚Äú◊©÷∏◊Å◊ë÷∑÷º◊™, shabbat,‚Äù which refers to ‚Äúthe completion of certain activity with which one had been occupied.‚Äù[11] After completing the creative transition from non-functional chaotic waters to a stable and functional temple, Elohim ‚Äúrests,‚Äù and the cosmos may now operate normally, with him in control.[12] The clearest Scriptural compilation of all these ideas occurs in Isa 66:1-2a:

This is what the Lord says:

‚ÄúThe heavens are my throne and the earth is my footstool. Where then is the house you will build for me? Where is the place where I will rest? My hand made them; that is how they came to be,‚Äù says the Lord.[13]

Doxologically, the creation of the universe as Elohim‚Äôs temple provides the impetus and parameters for appropriate worship of the Creator, whose ‚Äúmajestic splendor fills the entire earth‚Äù (Isa 6:3). As the only inhabitants of the cosmic temple created in the image (◊¶÷∂÷´◊ú÷∂◊ù, tselem) of Elohim, humans were created to function as ‚Äúpriest-kings‚Äù, extending his rule and dominion throughout the entire sacred realm of creation (Gen 1:26-28).[14] Through the lens of creation as temple, therefore, worship is relational, designed to be mediated by Elohim‚Äôs beloved priest-kings as they live in perfect relationship with him, with each other, and with the rest of creation, a full vision of the divine rest appropriate to Elohim‚Äôs cosmic temple.[15]

However, there is a tension between creation as it is now experienced and as it has just been described.[16] Genesis 3 gives the tragic explanation: Sin and Death, God‚Äôs cosmic enemies, have infected and affected every layer of the cosmic temple,[17] pulling the universe backwards, so to speak, toward the chaotic watery depths from which it was called into being. If the temple is no longer ‚Äúsafe,‚Äù is appropriate worship still possible?
