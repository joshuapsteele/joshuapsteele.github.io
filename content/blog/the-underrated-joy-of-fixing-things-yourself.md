I write software for a living. I enjoy it! But, a lot of the time, it's very abstract.

The things I work on live in databases, APIs, logs, pull requests, Jira tickets, and other people's screens. Even when something is "done," it is usually only done until the next bug report, edge case, feature request, or refactor.

Which is probably why I have found it so satisfying, lately, to fix physical things.

In the past few months, I've:

- Gotten the ice dispenser on our fridge working again
- Brought a dead string trimmer back to life (a Facebook Marketplace find that wouldn't start, fixed with a cheap carburetor and fuel line kit)
- Mostly killed the musty smell in our Honda Civic by cleaning out the AC system
- Replaced the broken door handle on our microwave

None of this is glamorous. I'm not restoring a farmhouse or rebuilding an engine over here.

But I have been surprised by how much I enjoy it. Here are three reasons why.

## 1. Every repair is a small, finishable education

I love learning, and a broken appliance is a nicely-sized course.

You don't have to become a refrigerator technician. You just have to figure out why *your* fridge isn't dispensing ice. You don't have to master small engine repair. You just have to learn enough about spark, fuel, and air to get *this* string trimmer running.

And, thankfully, a lot of this is just sitting there on YouTube.

It still feels kind of miraculous to me that, for almost any broken thing in my house, there is probably a video of someone taking apart that exact thing, or something close enough to count. Someone else has already figured out which plastic clip is going to snap if you pry in the wrong direction. Someone else has already filmed the awkward part where the spring flies across the room. God bless them.

Each repair leaves me knowing a little more about how some everyday object works. And that knowledge stacks up.

The string trimmer taught me things I'll use the next time a two-stroke engine refuses to start. The Civic's musty AC taught me a little bit about what is going on behind the dash. The microwave door handle taught me that some repair jobs are mostly about being patient and not losing screws.

That's not nothing!

## 2. It's concrete in a way my day job usually isn't

I spend a lot of my workday staring at a screen, trying to reason about invisible things. I like that work, but it can leave me feeling a little disembodied.

Repairing something is different.

The handle was broken. Now it isn't.

The ice dispenser didn't work. Now it does.

The car smelled gross. Now it smells less gross. (Progress!)

There is a before and after you can touch. You can point to the thing and say, "That was broken, and now it works." After a week of debugging software, that kind of blunt, physical success feels wonderful.

## 3. It rebuilds the "I can handle this" muscle

There is a small shift that happens after you fix a few things you previously would have paid someone else to fix, or just thrown away.

The default starts to change from "I guess we need to buy a new one" to "Let me at least take a look."

Sometimes, of course, the answer is still "call someone who knows what they're doing." Especially with electricity, gas, structural issues, etc. I am not trying to win a Darwin Award for DIY enthusiasm.

But a surprising amount of stuff is one part, one YouTube video, and one free afternoon away from working again.

And, yes, it saves money. The Facebook Marketplace string trimmer plus a cheap parts kit was much cheaper than buying new. But the money is only part of it.

The bigger thing is that the world feels a little less mysterious. I have a tiny bit more confidence that I can learn what I need to learn, solve the problem in front of me, and maybe keep one more thing out of the landfill.

I don't think you have to be especially handy to get started. I'm certainly not, though I'm trying to get there.

You need a broken thing, a few basic tools, a YouTube video made by someone more patient than you, and a willingness to be bad at something for a little while.

The first fix feels like luck. After a few more, it starts to feel like a skill.
